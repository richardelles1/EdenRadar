Solid in places, but with some consistent friction patterns. Here's what I'd actually change, in
  priority order:

  ---
  The Big Ones

  1. Role selection happens after login — it should happen before.
  Right now a new user lands, clicks Sign Up, enters their email, authenticates, and then gets asked
  "how will you use EdenRadar?" That's backwards. The portal choice should be the first decision, not
  an afterthought. It also frames the product better — someone landing on the site needs to understand
   "this is for me" before they enter their email. Move portal selection to the pre-auth step.

  2. Scout filters have two competing paradigms.
  There are single-select dropdowns and multi-select chip arrays for the same dimensions (stage,
  modality) — stage and stagesMulti both exist. A user who picks a stage from the dropdown and then
  tries the chip filter will get confused about which is active. Pick one model (multi-select chips
  are better for this corpus) and remove the other entirely.

  3. You can't cancel a search.
  Once you hit Search, patents and research start compiling and there's no abort. If you realize your
  query was wrong, you either wait or refresh. Given research searches can take 40s, that's a real
  frustration. A "Cancel" button on the compiling banners is a one-liner fix that has outsized UX
  impact.

  4. Data quality is invisible to users.
  A user looking at a sparse asset with a completeness score of 32 has no idea whether the thin
  content is because it's early-stage (legitimately), or because the scraper only got a title and the
  pipeline hasn't enriched it yet. The data_sparse flag exists in the DB but never surfaces in the UI.
   A simple "Limited data available" tag on low-completeness dossiers would set expectations and
  reduce confusion.

  ---
  Medium Priority

  5. No portal switcher is visible.
  The AppSwitcher component exists but appears buried in the sidebar footer. If a researcher wants to
  check EdenMarket, there's no obvious path. A persistent, visible switcher (or at minimum a clear
  link in the nav) would help, especially for multi-role users.

  6. Researcher and Discovery portals have no onboarding.
  IndustryOnboarding exists and fires on first login for industry users. The researcher and discovery
  portals have nothing equivalent. First-time users on those portals land in an empty state with no
  guidance.

  7. The dossier doesn't show enrichment provenance.
  Users can't tell whether the mechanism of action came from the original TTO page, a patent abstract,
   or was AI-inferred. The enrichment_sources JSONB column tracks this per-field — surfacing it (even
  as a small tooltip "AI inferred · Last updated 3 days ago") would meaningfully increase trust.

  8. Error recovery is passive.
  When patents or research fail to load, the banner says "unavailable" but offers no retry button. The
   user's only option is a full page refresh. Retry buttons on failed subsystems are trivial to add
  and save a lot of frustration.

  9. Scout to EdenMarket has no bridge.
  A user finds an interesting asset in Scout, wants to see if there's a deal listing for it in
  EdenMarket — there's no path. A "Check EdenMarket" affordance on asset cards (or in the dossier)
  would make the two products feel like one platform rather than two separate apps.

  ---
  Lower Priority but Worth Noting

  - Mobile nav loses context when the sidebar collapses — no breadcrumb or "you are here" indicator
  - Notes are under-discoverable — the collaboration feature has no visual count badge so users don't
  know it exists
  - Pipeline brief has no regenerate button — one-shot generation with no way to refine
  - The EdenMarket and main product have separate login flows — a logged-in Scout user shouldn't have
  to log in again for Market
  - "Compiling..." banners have no estimated time — even a rough "this usually takes ~20s" would
  reduce anxiety

  ---
  What's Already Good

  The pipeline/saved assets UX is genuinely well-done — inline notes, status management, team scoping,
   and export all feel polished. The alerts system is sophisticated (templates, live preview,
  pause/resume). And the loading states are reassuring rather than blocking. Those are real strengths
  worth preserving.

  The core problem is the platform is built for power users and doesn't have enough scaffolding for
  someone new. The value proposition is clear from the landing page, but the path from "I just signed
  up" to "I just found something useful" has too many unguided steps.
